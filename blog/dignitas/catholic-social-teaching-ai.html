Values Based AI Programming: History, Implementation, and Relation to Catholic Social Teaching

ZP
Zoltán Pap
2025 · DCF Board Member

The convergence of artificial intelligence with ethical frameworks represents one of the most critical challenges of our technological age. As AI systems increasingly influence human lives and societal structures, the need for values based programming becomes not merely desirable but essential. This exploration examines how Catholic Social Teaching provides a profound and comprehensive framework for approaching data sovereignty, sovereign AI, and values based AI programming—offering principles that transcend religious boundaries to address universal human concerns.

The Historical Foundation of Catholic Social Teaching

Catholic Social Teaching (CST) emerged formally in 1891 with Pope Leo XIII's encyclical "Rerum Novarum," responding to the Industrial Revolution's social upheavals. This wasn't merely a religious document but a comprehensive framework addressing the relationship between human dignity, technology, labor, and social organization. The timing was no coincidence—as mechanization transformed society, the Church articulated principles that remain remarkably relevant to our current AI revolution.

The core insight of CST is that all technological and economic systems must serve human dignity rather than reduce humans to mere components within those systems. This principle, developed through subsequent papal encyclicals and theological reflection, provides a robust foundation for addressing contemporary challenges in AI development.

Core Principles of Catholic Social Teaching Relevant to AI

Human Dignity as the Foundation

CST begins with the principle that every human person possesses inherent dignity that cannot be diminished or commodified. This dignity isn't earned or achieved—it's intrinsic to human existence. For AI systems, this means programming must recognize and protect this dignity, ensuring that algorithms never reduce humans to mere data points or optimization targets.

In practical terms, this translates to AI systems that:

  • Respect individual privacy and autonomy
  • Avoid manipulative or exploitative practices
  • Maintain transparency in decision-making processes affecting human lives
  • Preserve meaningful human agency and choice

The Common Good and Solidarity

CST emphasizes that individual flourishing occurs within community contexts. The principle of the common good requires that technological advancement benefit all members of society, not just privileged segments. Solidarity demands that we recognize our fundamental interconnectedness and shared responsibility for one another's wellbeing.

For AI development, this means:

  • Ensuring equitable access to AI benefits
  • Preventing algorithmic discrimination
  • Designing systems that strengthen rather than fragment communities
  • Considering global impacts, not just local optimizations

Subsidiarity and Distributed Authority

The principle of subsidiarity holds that decisions should be made at the most local level capable of addressing them effectively. This principle directly challenges centralized AI control models, advocating instead for distributed governance structures that preserve local autonomy while enabling coordination when necessary.

This principle supports:

  • Decentralized AI architectures
  • Local data sovereignty
  • Community-level AI governance
  • Preservation of cultural and regional diversity in AI systems

Data Sovereignty Through a CST Lens

Data sovereignty—the principle that individuals and communities should control their own data—aligns perfectly with CST's emphasis on human dignity and subsidiarity. Just as CST recognizes property rights as extensions of human dignity (while maintaining social obligations), data sovereignty recognizes data rights as fundamental to human autonomy in the digital age.

CST's approach to property—that it carries both rights and responsibilities—provides a nuanced framework for data governance. Data isn't merely a commodity to be extracted and monetized; it's an extension of human personality and community life that must be stewarded responsibly.

The Preferential Option for the Poor

CST's "preferential option for the poor" requires that we evaluate systems by how they affect the most vulnerable. In the context of data sovereignty, this means:

  • Protecting vulnerable populations from data exploitation
  • Ensuring that data-driven systems don't perpetuate existing inequalities
  • Designing AI systems that actively work to reduce rather than amplify disparities
  • Guaranteeing that those with limited technical resources aren't excluded from data sovereignty

Sovereign AI and National/Cultural Identity

Sovereign AI—the development of AI systems that reflect and protect national or cultural values—resonates with CST's recognition of legitimate cultural diversity and the importance of local identity within universal human solidarity. CST has long defended the rights of nations and cultures to maintain their distinctive characteristics while participating in global community.

This balance between particular identity and universal values offers a framework for sovereign AI that avoids both techno-imperialism and isolationist fragmentation. Sovereign AI systems should:

  • Reflect local values while respecting universal human dignity
  • Preserve linguistic and cultural diversity
  • Enable international cooperation without sacrificing autonomy
  • Protect against digital colonization

Values Based AI Programming: A Practical Framework

Embedding Values in Code

Values based AI programming requires translating abstract ethical principles into concrete computational constraints and objectives. CST's systematic approach to ethics—moving from principles to prudential judgments to specific applications—provides a model for this translation process.

The implementation process involves:

  1. Principle Articulation: Clearly defining the ethical principles guiding development
  2. Operational Translation: Converting principles into measurable objectives and constraints
  3. Implementation Design: Creating architectures that embody these values
  4. Continuous Evaluation: Monitoring outcomes against intended values
  5. Iterative Refinement: Adjusting systems based on real-world impacts

The Role of Virtue Ethics

CST's integration of virtue ethics offers insights for AI development that go beyond rule-based approaches. Virtues like prudence, justice, temperance, and fortitude suggest AI systems that don't merely follow rules but embody patterns of excellent judgment.

This virtue-based approach suggests AI systems that:

  • Exercise prudent judgment in complex situations
  • Balance competing goods rather than optimizing single metrics
  • Demonstrate consistency and reliability (fortitude)
  • Avoid excess in any particular direction (temperance)

Historical Parallels and Lessons

The Catholic Church's response to previous technological revolutions offers instructive parallels. During the Industrial Revolution, CST emerged to address the dehumanizing effects of mechanization while acknowledging technology's potential benefits. This balanced approach—neither technophobic nor uncritically enthusiastic—provides a model for engaging with AI.

Key historical lessons include:

  • The importance of proactive ethical engagement rather than reactive regulation
  • The need for international cooperation in addressing global technological challenges
  • The danger of allowing economic efficiency to override human dignity
  • The value of diverse voices in shaping technological development

Implementation Strategies

Institutional Frameworks

CST's emphasis on institutions as mediating structures between individuals and larger systems suggests the need for new institutional forms to govern AI development. These might include:

  • Ethics review boards with genuine authority over AI development
  • Multi-stakeholder governance bodies including affected communities
  • International frameworks respecting both sovereignty and universal principles
  • Educational institutions preparing citizens for AI governance participation

Economic Considerations

CST's economic teachings—emphasizing that economies should serve people rather than the reverse—challenge current AI development models dominated by profit maximization. A CST-informed approach would prioritize:

  • Cooperative and mutual ownership models for AI systems
  • Fair distribution of AI-generated wealth
  • Protection of human labor and meaningful work
  • Economic structures that support human flourishing over efficiency alone

Challenges and Responses

The Complexity Challenge

Critics might argue that CST's principles are too abstract or complex for practical AI implementation. However, CST's long tradition of practical application demonstrates that complex ethical principles can indeed guide concrete action. The key lies in developing intermediate principles and prudential judgments that bridge abstract values and specific implementations.

The Pluralism Challenge

In pluralistic societies, some question whether religious frameworks like CST can guide public AI development. However, CST's principles—human dignity, solidarity, subsidiarity—resonate across religious and secular boundaries. These aren't exclusively Catholic ideas but contributions to humanity's shared ethical heritage.

The Technical Challenge

Translating ethical principles into code remains technically challenging. However, recent advances in value alignment, interpretable AI, and constitutional AI demonstrate that technical solutions can embody ethical principles. CST's systematic approach to ethics provides a framework for tackling these challenges methodically.

Future Directions

Toward Integral AI Development

CST's concept of integral human development—addressing all dimensions of human flourishing—suggests an approach to AI that goes beyond narrow optimization. Integral AI development would consider:

  • Spiritual and psychological wellbeing, not just material outcomes
  • Community relationships and social capital
  • Environmental sustainability and intergenerational justice
  • Cultural preservation and development

The Role of Civil Society

CST's emphasis on civil society as a crucial space between state and market suggests that religious organizations, NGOs, community groups, and other civil society actors must play central roles in shaping AI development. These organizations bring:

  • Moral authority and ethical expertise
  • Connection to affected communities
  • Long-term perspective beyond political and economic cycles
  • International networks for global coordination

Practical Applications

Healthcare AI

In healthcare, CST principles would guide AI systems that:

  • Prioritize patient dignity and autonomy
  • Ensure equitable access to AI-enhanced care
  • Preserve the human dimension of healing relationships
  • Respect life from conception to natural death

Educational AI

Educational AI informed by CST would:

  • Enhance rather than replace human teachers
  • Respect diverse learning styles and cultural contexts
  • Promote integral human formation, not just skill acquisition
  • Ensure equal access regardless of economic status

Economic AI

Economic AI systems guided by CST would:

  • Consider social costs and benefits, not just financial returns
  • Protect workers' rights and dignity
  • Promote inclusive economic participation
  • Respect the universal destination of goods

Conclusion: A Universal Framework for Human-Centered AI

Catholic Social Teaching offers more than religious perspective on AI development—it provides a comprehensive, time-tested framework for ensuring technology serves human flourishing. Its principles of human dignity, common good, subsidiarity, and solidarity address the fundamental challenges of data sovereignty, sovereign AI, and values based programming.

The framework's strength lies not in prescribing specific technical solutions but in providing principles flexible enough to guide development across diverse contexts while maintaining focus on what matters most: human dignity and flourishing. As we stand at this crucial juncture in human history, CST's wisdom—developed over centuries of engaging with technological and social change—offers invaluable guidance for creating AI systems that truly serve humanity.

The path forward requires collaboration across religious, cultural, and national boundaries, united by shared commitment to human dignity. CST provides a foundation for this collaboration—not as an imposed framework but as a contribution to humanity's collective wisdom about technology and human flourishing. The principles it articulates resonate far beyond Catholic communities, offering insights valuable to all who seek to ensure that artificial intelligence enhances rather than diminishes human dignity.

As we develop increasingly powerful AI systems, the question isn't whether to embed values in our technology—values are always present, whether acknowledged or not. The question is which values will guide development and how we'll ensure those values truly serve human flourishing. Catholic Social Teaching, with its rich intellectual tradition and practical wisdom, provides essential resources for answering these questions in ways that honor both human dignity and technological progress.

The future of AI is not predetermined. Through conscious choice and deliberate action, guided by ethical frameworks like CST, we can create AI systems that reflect our highest aspirations for human society—systems that protect dignity, promote solidarity, respect subsidiarity, and serve the common good. This is not merely a technical challenge but a profoundly human one, requiring the best of our moral imagination and practical wisdom. Catholic Social Teaching, properly understood and applied, offers a beacon of light for this crucial journey.
