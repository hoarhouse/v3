Faith Leaders

Towards Humanity's Higher Good

DCF
Domus Communis Foundation
2025

The choice of a papal name is rarely accidental. By taking the name Leo XIV, the new Pope evokes the memory of Leo XIII, whose 1891 encyclical Rerum Novarum helped the Church respond to the upheavals of the industrial revolution. If that earlier Leo addressed the social consequences of steam and steel, today the challenge may lie in navigating the world being reshaped by algorithms, data, and artificial intelligence.

It is still early in the pontificate of Leo XIV, and the contours of his engagement with AI are only beginning to emerge. Yet the questions are already clear. Artificial intelligence promises extraordinary advances—from breakthroughs in medical diagnosis and drug discovery to new tools for climate modeling and environmental protection. At the same time, it raises profound concerns about the future of work, the concentration of economic power, and the potential use of autonomous systems in warfare.

Few technologies have ever carried such a mixture of promise and risk.

A Pope Who Understands Science

Leo XIV approaches these questions with an unusual background. Before entering the priesthood he studied mathematics, giving him a familiarity with scientific reasoning that may prove significant in conversations about emerging technologies. This does not mean the Church will suddenly become a technical authority on artificial intelligence. But it suggests that the new pontiff may be particularly attentive to the deeper implications of technologies whose logic often escapes public understanding.

At a moment when debates about AI frequently oscillate between utopian optimism and dystopian fear, Leo XIV's scientific formation may help frame the discussion in more grounded terms—recognizing both the extraordinary capabilities of these systems and the limits of purely technical solutions to human problems.

Between Risk and Promise

The stakes are not abstract. Artificial intelligence is already reshaping global labor markets, raising fears that automation could displace entire categories of work. Military applications of AI—from autonomous weapons to algorithmic decision systems—have also intensified concerns about the future of war. Meanwhile, the enormous computational power required to train large AI models raises new environmental questions about energy use and digital infrastructure.

Yet the same technologies could also become powerful tools for the common good. AI is already accelerating medical research, improving diagnostics, and helping scientists model complex environmental systems such as climate change. Used responsibly, these tools could expand access to healthcare, improve disaster prediction, and support sustainable development across the world.

The challenge, therefore, is not simply technological but moral: how to ensure that these systems serve humanity rather than reshape it in ways that undermine dignity, solidarity, and justice.

An Augustinian Perspective

Leo XIV's spiritual formation may also shape the way he approaches these questions. As a member of the Augustinian tradition, his intellectual heritage is deeply influenced by Saint Augustine, who reflected intensely on the relationship between knowledge, power, and the human heart.

Augustine famously warned that knowledge without wisdom risks becoming a form of domination rather than a path toward truth. In the context of artificial intelligence, such insights may resonate with renewed relevance. Technologies capable of processing vast amounts of data cannot replace the moral discernment that guides human judgment.

For Augustine, the ultimate question was not what humans could build, but what kind of people they were becoming.

A Moral Conversation Just Beginning

If Leo XIII helped the Church confront the social consequences of industrial capitalism, Leo XIV may find himself presiding over a moment when humanity must reflect on the moral implications of digital civilization.

Artificial intelligence will not determine humanity's future on its own. But it will shape the conditions under which that future unfolds.

The real question, therefore, may not be whether machines become more intelligent—but whether humanity remains wise enough to guide them.

— Alessio Pecorario
