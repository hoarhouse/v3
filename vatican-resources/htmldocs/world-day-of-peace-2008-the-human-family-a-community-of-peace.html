The Human Family, A Community of Peace

41st World Day of Peace Message

Pope Benedict XVI
January 1, 2008
15 Paragraphs

Introduction

1. At the beginning of a New Year, I wish to send my fervent good wishes for peace, together with a heartfelt message of hope to men and women throughout the world. I do so by offering for our common reflection the theme which I have placed at the beginning of this message. It is one which I consider particularly important: the human family, a community of peace. The first form of communion between persons is that born of the love of a man and a woman who decide to enter a stable union in order to build together a new family. But the peoples of the earth, too, are called to build relationships of solidarity and cooperation among themselves, as befits members of the one human family: "All peoples"as the Second Vatican Council declared"are one community and have one origin, because God caused the whole human race to dwell on the face of the earth (cf. Acts 17:26); they also have one final end, God".

The Natural Family

2. The natural family, as an intimate communion of life and love, based on marriage between a man and a woman, constitutes "the primary place of 'humanization' for the person and society", and a "cradle of life and love". The family is therefore rightly defined as the first natural society, "a divine institution that stands at the foundation of life of the human person as the prototype of every social order".

3. Indeed, in a healthy family life we experience some of the fundamental elements of peace: justice and love between brothers and sisters, the role of authority expressed by parents, loving concern for the members who are weaker because of youth, sickness or old age, mutual help in the necessities of life, readiness to accept others and, if necessary, to forgive them. For this reason, the family is the first and indispensable teacher of peace. It is no wonder, therefore, that violence, if perpetrated in the family, is seen as particularly intolerable. Consequently, when it is said that the family is "the primary living cell of society", something essential is being stated. The family is the foundation of society for this reason too: because it enables its members in decisive ways to experience peace. It follows that the human community cannot do without the service provided by the family.

Key Teaching: The family is the first and indispensable teacher of peace. Where can young people gradually learn to savour the genuine "taste" of peace better than in the original "nest" which nature prepares for them?

4. Because of its importance in relation to society, the family has been endowed with numerous rights which are enumerated in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. The Holy See, for its part, desired to acknowledge the special dignity of the family by publishing a Charter of the Rights of the Family. The Preamble states: "The rights of the person, even though they are expressed as rights of the individual, have a fundamental social dimension which finds an innate and vital expression in the family". The rights set forth in the Charter are an expression and an explicitation of the natural law, inscribed in the human heart and revealed to humanity by reason and by the word of God. The denial or even the restriction of the rights of the family, by obscuring the truth about man, threatens the very foundations of peace.

5. Consequently, whoever, even unknowingly, circumvents the institution of the family undermines peace in the entire community, national and international, since he weakens what is in effect the primary agency of peace. This point merits special reflection: everything that serves to weaken the family based on the marriage of a man and woman, everything that directly or indirectly stands in the way of its openness to the responsible acceptance of a new life, everything that obstructs its right to be primarily responsible for the education of its children, constitutes an objective obstacle on the road to peace. The family needs to have a home, employment and a just recognition of the domestic activity of parents, the possibility of schooling for children, and basic health care for all. When society and public policy are not committed to assisting the family in these areas, they deprive themselves of an essential resource in the service of peace.

Humanity as One Great Family

6. The social community, if it is to live in peace, is also called to draw inspiration from the values on which the family community is based. This is as true for local communities as it is for national communities; it is also true for the international community itself, for the human family which dwells in that common house which is the earth. Here, however, we cannot forget that the family comes into being from the responsible and definitive "yes" of a man and a women, and it continues to live from the conscious "yes" of the children who gradually join it. The family community, in order to prosper, needs the generous consent of all its members. This realization also needs to become a shared conviction on the part of all those called to form the common human family. We need to say our own "yes" to this vocation which God has inscribed in our very nature. We do not live alongside one another purely by chance; all of us are progressing along a common path as men and women, and thus as brothers and sisters. Consequently, it is essential that we should all be committed to living our lives in an attitude of responsibility before God, acknowledging him as the deepest source of our own existence and that of others. By going back to this supreme principle we are able to perceive the unconditional worth of each human being, and thus to lay the premises for building a humanity at peace. Without this transcendent foundation society is a mere aggregation of neighbours, not a community of brothers and sisters called to form one great family.

"We do not live alongside one another purely by chance; all of us are progressing along a common path as men and women, and thus as brothers and sisters."

Family, Human Community and Environment

7. The family needs a home, a fit environment in which to develop its proper relationships. For the human family, this home is the earth, the environment that God the Creator has given us to inhabit with creativity and responsibility. We need to care for the environment: it has been entrusted to men and women to be protected and cultivated with responsible freedom, with the good of all as a constant guiding criterion. Human beings, obviously, are of supreme worth vis--vis creation as a whole. Respecting the environment does not mean considering material or animal nature more important than man. Rather, it means not selfishly considering nature to be at the complete disposal of our own interests, for future generations also have the right to reap its benefits and to exhibit towards nature the same responsible freedom that we claim for ourselves. Nor must we overlook the poor, who are excluded in many cases from the goods of creation destined for all. Humanity today is rightly concerned about the ecological balance of tomorrow. It is important for assessments in this regard to be carried out prudently, in dialogue with wise people and experts, without ideological pressure to draw hasty conclusions, and above all with the aim of reaching agreement on a model of sustainable development capable of ensuring the well-being of all while respecting environmental balances.

8. If the protection of the environment involves costs, they should be justly distributed, taking due account of the different levels of development of various countries and the need for solidarity with future generations. Prudence does not mean failing to accept responsibilities and postponing decisions; it means being committed to making joint decisions after pondering responsibly the road to be taken, decisions aimed at strengthening that covenant between human beings and the environment, which should mirror the creative love of God, from whom we come and towards whom we are journeying.

Environmental Responsibility: Respecting the environment means not selfishly considering nature to be at the complete disposal of our own interests, for future generations also have the right to reap its benefits.

Economic Life and Solidarity

9. The family, the human community and the environment: these are the three contexts in which I suggested we consider the theme of peace for this World Day of Peace. In a certain sense, the first element is also connected to the third. In a family, a shared project is required, as is patience and trust. These same values are indispensable for shaping a community truly directed towards the common good and towards peace. With regard to this, it may be useful to recall that loveas Saint Paul said in his hymn to charity (cf. 1 Cor 13:1-13)is the supreme value which embraces all the others. This is true also in the social sphere: "Charity", it has been said, "is the principle not only of micro-relationships (with friends, with family members or within small groups) but also of macro-relationships (social, economic and political ones)".

10. On this point, the social doctrine of the Catholic Church can offer concrete assistance. For example, it repeatedly calls attention to characteristics of three levels of the common good that must be respected and promoted if peace is to be established: the good of individual persons, of families and of intermediate groups; the good of a particular State and of the citizens who make it up; the good of the human family and of all humanity. As the world becomes ever more globalized, it is increasingly clear that the good of one individual nation or ethnic group cannot be pursued in isolation from the good of humanity as a whole. This awareness has contributed significantly to the emergence of an international public authority at the service of peoples and nations, one with the task of maintaining at the world level those common goods which individual States are unable to promote and protect on their own.

The Importance of Moral Law

11. Members of the family, since they live together, need norms to guide them; the same is true of the community of peoples. Among other things, these norms should inhibit that "selfish individualism" against which, as was noted earlier, families and nations must protect themselves. In the inner sanctuary of conscience, individuals discover a law that they do not give themselves, but which they are called to obey. This law is not merely a matter of human convention, for it has its origin in God himself; it is written on the heart of every person (cf. Rom 2:15) and all are called to bear witness to it. This natural law is the ultimate foundation of the juridic order and of social life itself. It reminds us that there are certain basic rights and duties which not even the State can grant or take away, since they precede the very existence of the State. When this foundation is lacking, the authority of the State is weakened and civil coexistence is threatened. It is from this moral law that juridic rules derive the greater part of their capacity to obligate.

12. This moral obligation does not rest on force alone, but rather on the respect due to the intrinsic dignity of every individual. The recognition of this dignity implies the affirmation of the universality and indivisibility of human rights. It cannot be emphasized enough how crucial it is, for the sake of peace, that a consensus on the universality and indivisibility of these rights be retrieved and strengthened. A vision of rights that is purely relativistic would deny their universality in the name of respect for cultural, political, social and religious differences. Respect for the legitimate diversity of cultures must not lead to the rejection of shared basic values, for these values are rooted in the nature of the person; they are the hallmarks of authentic humanity.

13. The family, too, has need of a home, in which it can live and grow. That home is the law. The law is the primary guarantee of respect for the rights and duties of individuals and communities. In our day, humanity has made considerable progress in the area of international law, yet much more needs to be accomplished. There is an urgent need to complete the process of codifying and effectively implementing international humanitarian law. There is also an urgent need for international law to be brought to bear more justly and firmly on those new problems raised by the exploitation of the earth's resources and by issues of biotechnology. Adequate protection must be ensured for those men and women who are most vulnerable and marginalized. We need only think of the condition of refugees and displaced persons, whose number is tragically on the rise; or of all those who are victims of intolerance and discrimination for religious, political, racial and other reasons.

Overcoming Conflicts and Disarmament

14. Humanity today is unfortunately experiencing great division and sharp conflicts which cast dark shadows on its future. Vast areas of the world are caught up in situations of increasing tension, while the danger of an increase in the number of countries possessing nuclear weapons causes justifiable apprehension in every responsible person. Many civil wars are still being fought in Africa, even though a number of countries there have made progress on the road to freedom and democracy. The Middle East is still a theatre of conflict and violence, which also affects neighbouring nations and regions and risks drawing them into a spiral of violence. On a more general level, one must acknowledge with regret the continuing growth in military expenditure and the flourishing arms trade, while the political and juridic process established by the international community for promoting disarmament is bogged down in general indifference. How can there ever be a future of peace when investments are still made in the production of arms and in research aimed at developing new ones? It can only be hoped that the international community will find the wisdom and courage to take up once more, jointly and with renewed conviction, the process of disarmament, and thus concretely ensure the right to peace enjoyed by every individual and every people. By their commitment to safeguarding the good of peace, the various agencies of the international community will regain the authority which is indispensable for making their initiatives credible and effective.

Call for Nuclear Disarmament

The first to benefit from decisive choices for disarmament will be the poor countries, which rightly claim, after having been promised development assistance, the concrete implementation of that assistance.

Final Appeal

15. In my Christmas Message, I expressed the wish that "people everywhere, by their commitment to the great common values of peace, will be able, with the aid of true wisdom and persevering good will, to face together the difficult challenges already present and those looming on the horizon". At the beginning of the New Year, I would like once more to call attention to two anniversaries: the sixtieth anniversary of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights and the twenty-fifth anniversary of the Holy See's publication of the Charter of the Rights of the Family. Both documents were aimed at helping humanity to advance along the road of progress and civility. But that road is still a long one. With these thoughts, I invoke God's blessings upon all of you and upon all those dear to you; I ask him to inspire each of you with courage and perseverance in building "the civilization of love".

Final Message: I invite all to join with me in praying to God, so that the human family, through the cultivation of the values of peace, will be able to face the challenges of our age with wisdom and strength. May the light and strength of Jesus grant believers the energy to work tirelessly for the coming of the civilization of authentic and lasting peace.

From the Vatican, 8 December 2007
BENEDICTUS PP. XVI
